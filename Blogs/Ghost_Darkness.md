I was watching the 1999 film The Ghost and the Darkness starring Val Kilmer, from the comfort of my living room. Back in the day, I remember renting it from the local video shop. Kilmer was a big star at the time, having recently appeared in Batman Forever, which was a fairly good offering and quite enjoyable, though a bit over the top.

What startled me during the film was hearing Kilmer’s character, John Henry Patterson, describe himself as Irish. This puzzled me since I was unaware of Patterson's Irish background. As it turns out, the story is based on the book The Man-Eaters of Tsavo. Patterson, portrayed by Kilmer, was an Anglo-Irish military engineer tasked with building railways in Kenya. His life and role are fascinating, which I’ll elaborate on shortly.

The film also features Michael Douglas, likely added to increase star power and make the movie a more irresistible draw at the box office. Despite its flaws, The Ghost and the Darkness deserves credit for being refreshingly different from the typical films of the ’90s. Set amidst man-eating lions in colonial Kenya, the movie made a genuine attempt to explore unique ideas and themes that set it apart from other mid-’90s offerings.

Kilmer delivered a subdued performance, allowing Douglas to take on the more charismatic role. While the movie is far from perfect, it’s worth acknowledging its originality and ambition. However, it suffers from the gap between audience expectations and what it actually delivers. This mismatch can sometimes work against an otherwise adequate film.

On a historical note, John Henry Patterson was indeed Irish, hailing from Longford, near Ballymahon (Forgney). He was a committed Zionist and, after World War I, became the founder of the Jewish Legion. He is even credited as one of the forefathers of the Israeli Defense Forces (IDF), which is ironic considering the prevalent pro-Palestinian sentiment in Ireland.

Additionally, Patterson was a staunch loyalist and one of the founders of the Ulster Volunteer Force (UVF). He was friends with Benzion Netanyahu, the father of Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu. In fact, Netanyahu’s father named his eldest son, Jonathan, partially after Patterson. Jonathan Netanyahu, a famous Israeli military commander, died during the Entebbe raid and is considered a national hero in Israel.

It’s perhaps due to these historical nuances that Patterson isn’t well-known in Ireland. People in Ballymahon were unaware that Val Kilmer played one of their own in the movie. Patterson’s death also went relatively unreported in Irish news.

While The Ghost and the Darkness isn’t the best movie I’ve ever seen, I was fascinated by its originality and intellectual ambition. It’s a film that stands out, even with its flaws.
