Ladies and gentlemen,

We gather today to honour the life of Gerry—a woman of grace, intellect, and quiet strength. It’s hard to capture someone so richly layered in a single tribute, but I hope these words offer a glimpse into the remarkable life she led and the legacy she leaves behind.

Gerry was born and raised in Irish Town and Plaudstown, places that shaped her early sense of belonging and rootedness. She carried those landscapes with her, even as her journey took her far beyond them. At Trinity College, she cultivated a love for language and literature that would become a lifelong passion. That love led her to England, where she taught at Mulberry School for Girls in East London—a place she spoke of with pride and affection.

She lived in Layton and held fond memories of Islington, Whitechapel, and Mile End—neighbourhoods that became part of her personal map of London. For Gerry, London wasn’t just a city; it was a living archive of stories, theatres, and gardens. She attended Shakespearean plays at the Actors’ Church and in the garden, and delighted in the Pride French Festival. Her vocabulary was expansive, her wit sharp, and her crosswords—always completed in pen—were legendary.

Gerry had a gift for language, not just in its technical mastery, but in its emotional resonance. She once recited Jenny Joseph’s “Warning” upon seeing someone in a purple shawl and red beret—a moment that sparked laughter and recognition, especially with my mother. She loved Emily Dickinson and could turn a casual conversation into a poetic reflection. Her intellect was matched by a light-hearted, authentic spirit that made her both formidable and deeply endearing.

She loved animals, especially her dogs Kira and Pippin, who were more than companions—they were family. Her connection to Johnstown remained strong throughout her life, and in 1995, she returned to Ireland to care for a seriously ill aunt, a gesture of devotion that spoke volumes about her character.

In later years, Gerry lived in France, pursued genealogy, and helped piece together our family tree. She spent time in Saint Camillus’s Nursing Home, a place she had often passed while caring for her elderly parents. Even in those quieter chapters, she remained curious, engaged, and full of stories.

Gerry was well-versed in English history and historical fiction, and she loved hearing about trips to England—especially tales from the Mapp and Lucia Festival. She explored places she considered “home,” not just geographically, but emotionally and intellectually.

To remember Gerry is to remember a woman who lived with elegance and conviction. She taught us that language is not just a tool, but a bridge—between people, places, and generations. She showed us that care can be quiet and steadfast, and that memory is something we build together.

May we carry her stories forward, speak her name often, and honour her by living with the same curiosity, grace, and love she so generously gave.

Thank you.
